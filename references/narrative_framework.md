# Narrative Framework: Senior UX Case Study Stories

## The story structure (in order)

Every case study follows this sequence. Each beat must be present. Do not reorder.

1. **Business problem** — What was at stake for the business, and why it mattered. Revenue, retention, competitive pressure, operational cost. State this before any design work is mentioned.
2. **Why it was hard** — The real obstacle. At senior levels this is almost always organizational: fragmented ownership, misaligned stakeholders, undefined success criteria, competing priorities. Name it specifically.
3. **What you did to navigate it** — The work that doesn't show up in a portfolio. Alignment, reframing, negotiation, process changes. This is where senior-level judgment lives.
4. **The design and how you validated it** — The actual design decisions, and the evidence you used to evaluate them. Present testing data with rigor: methodology, sample size, benchmarks, iteration results.
5. **The metrics gap (if applicable)** — Own it before the interviewer asks. Acknowledge what you don't have, explain why, pivot to what you do know, and make a transparent estimate if the inputs support one.
6. **What you would do with more ownership** — End on strategic thinking. What would you instrument, change, or push for if you controlled the roadmap? This is forward-looking and shows systems thinking.

---

## Seniority calibration

The same project story needs different emphasis depending on the level being interviewed for. Adjust the weight given to each beat accordingly.

| Level | Emphasize | De-emphasize |
|---|---|---|
| **Senior Product Designer** | Design decisions and validation. Show clear thinking and solid process. | Organizational politics. Keep complexity present but not the centerpiece. |
| **Lead Product Designer** | Cross-functional navigation. How you got teams aligned, shifted priorities, unblocked work. | Individual craft details. The design itself is secondary to how you moved the org. |
| **Staff Product Designer** | Strategic framing of the business problem. Why this project mattered relative to other work. Risk you identified or prevented. | Execution details. The interviewer assumes you can execute. |
| **Principal Product Designer** | Systems thinking. How this project connected to broader platform or organizational health. What you'd change about how the company approaches this class of problem. | The project itself. It's a lens, not the point. |

## Handling the metrics gap

This is the most common credibility threat in senior UX interviews. The candidate has strong design signal but no production analytics to validate the outcome.

**Do not** wait for the interviewer to notice. Own it in beat 5, using this structure:

1. State the gap plainly. ("We didn't have production analytics on these screens.")
2. Explain why, in one sentence. ("The instrumentation wasn't in place.")
3. Pivot to what you do know. Restate the testing evidence and the scale of the user base affected.
4. Make a transparent estimate if the math supports it. Use conditional framing: "If X held in production..." Never present an estimate as a fact.
5. Close by saying what you would have instrumented. Name specific metrics. This shows you understand measurement as a system.

---

## Tone and language rules

- **Conversational, not formal.** This is spoken narrative, not a written report. Write it the way someone would actually say it in a room.
- **Specific, not abstract.** "Three backend systems owned by separate teams" not "complex organizational dynamics." "38% completion rate" not "low completion."
- **Honest, not apologetic.** Acknowledge limitations without self-criticism. "I don't know" is fine. "Unfortunately I wasn't able to..." is not.
- **No hedging on the design.** Be direct about what you decided and why. Reserve uncertainty for outcomes you genuinely can't confirm.
- **No meta-commentary.** Do not explain why you're structuring the story a certain way. Just tell the story.

## Annotated example

The story is on the left. The annotation explains what each beat is doing and why.

---

**"So I was brought onto a project at FinPay — B2B payments platform — to redesign their new business onboarding flow."**
→ *Beat 1 setup. Company, domain, project scope in one sentence. No preamble.*

**"And the reason this project landed on anyone's radar is that the business was bleeding prospects at a very specific, very fixable point."**
→ *Beat 1. Business problem stated before any design is mentioned. "Bleeding prospects" is concrete. "Very specific, very fixable" creates tension — it implies the problem should have been solved already.*

**"Sales had noticed it first. Prospects who actually completed account setup were converting to paid at a strong rate. But a meaningful chunk were dropping out during configuration — and support was fielding the same three tickets over and over about the same steps."**
→ *Beat 1 continued. Two independent sources confirm the problem. This isn't your hypothesis — it's organizational signal. It also establishes the problem was already known, which makes it clearly worth solving.*

**"So the question wasn't whether this was worth solving. It was obvious. The question was why nobody had fixed it yet."**
→ *Transition to beat 2. Reframes the story. Creates curiosity about what made it hard.*

**"The answer was that the flow touched three backend systems, each owned by a different team. Nobody had full visibility into what a user actually experienced end-to-end."**
→ *Beat 2. The real obstacle is organizational, not technical. Naming it specifically shows you understand where friction lives in large companies.*

**"One of the first things I did — before I touched a single wireframe — was get those teams into a room and establish a shared definition of what 'onboarding completion' even meant. That sounds simple. It took three weeks."**
→ *Beat 3. "Before I touched a single wireframe" positions alignment as the first priority. The admission that it took three weeks is honest and makes the next line land.*

**"But it also meant that when we shipped, the product team had a shared basis for prioritizing onboarding in the next roadmap cycle. That was a real outcome."**
→ *Beat 3 continued. Claims organizational impact — influence on how the org works, not just the artifact shipped. Senior interviewers weight this heavily.*

**"On the design side, I ran usability testing at three points — baseline, mid-iteration, and final. Structured sessions, think-aloud, task-based, with clear pass/fail criteria on each critical step."**
→ *Beat 4. Methodology stated before results. "Baseline, mid-iteration, and final" shows iterative discipline. "Pass/fail criteria" shows success was defined before testing.*

**"At baseline, completion on the three problem steps was around 38%. By the final round it was 89%. Time on task for configuration dropped from 14 minutes to six. And unprompted confusion went from six out of eight to one out of eight."**
→ *Beat 4 continued. Three metrics, each tracking a different dimension: completion, efficiency, cognitive load. "Around 38%" adds honesty rather than false precision.*

**"Now — I want to be straightforward about something. We didn't have production analytics on these screens. The instrumentation simply wasn't there. So I can't tell you what happened after we shipped."**
→ *Beat 5. Gap owned before the interviewer asks. "I want to be straightforward" signals honesty, not defensiveness. Stating the gap plainly prevents the feeling of something being omitted.*

**"What I can tell you is that the testing signal was consistent across rounds, the failure modes were clearly identified, and the flow served roughly 400 new accounts a month."**
→ *Beat 5 continued. Three factual statements that rebuild confidence. Consistent = reliable. Failure modes identified = you understood the problem. 400 accounts = not a small experiment.*

**"If that completion rate held — and there was no reason to think it wouldn't — we're talking about 50 to 80 fewer accounts stalling monthly. At FinPay's ACV, that's a number worth instrumenting."**
→ *Beat 5 continued. Conditional framing makes it clearly a projection. The math is simple and followable. "Worth instrumenting" positions the estimate as a reason to measure, not a substitute for it.*

**"Which is exactly what I pushed for. I flagged to the product team that any future onboarding work needed step-level drop-off tracking as a baseline before we could measure ROI."**
→ *Beat 6. Ends on strategic thinking. You didn't just identify the gap — you advocated for fixing it. Shows you think about measurement as a system.*

**"That conversation happened. Whether it made it into the next sprint — I don't know. But that's what I'd have done differently if I owned the roadmap."**
→ *Beat 6 close. "I don't know" is credible, not weak. "What I'd have done differently" shows ownership without self-criticism. Clean, forward-looking close.*
